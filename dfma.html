Design for Manufacture and Assembly

Moving construction from fragmented site work toward productized, manufacturable building systems.

DfMA treats buildings as assemblies shaped by production, logistics, tolerance, sequencing, and lifecycle value.

Construction Needs a Production Logic

DfMA moves construction thinking upstream, linking architectural intent with modules, interfaces, supply chains, factory capability, craneage, sequencing, carbon, cost, and adaptability.

Traditional construction relies heavily on fragmented on-site work, variable labour conditions, rework, waste, and late coordination. These problems are often symptoms of designs that were not prepared for manufacture, transport, and assembly.

Our research explores how computational design, graph learning, optimisation, expert knowledge, and human-AI collaboration can help teams generate and evaluate manufacturable building options at early design stages.

Hong Kong Hospitals as a DfMA Testbed

Hong Kong's hospital estate makes the DfMA challenge concrete: a large development pipeline, ageing demand, constrained capacity, and complex healthcare infrastructure.

In 2016, the government launched the first 10-year Hospital Development Plan, allocating HK$200 billion for 16 projects to provide more than 5,000 additional beds and 90 operating theatres. In 2021, the Hospital Authority was invited to prepare the second plan, covering 18 hospital projects and one community health centre project, with an estimated budget of HK$270 billion and a target of over 9,000 additional beds.

By 2050, people aged 65 and above will account for 40.6% of Hong Kong's population, yet there will be only 4.3 hospital beds per 1,000 people, compared with 12.6 in Japan. Elderly patients often require longer stays, intensive MEP systems, and specialised wards such as ICUs and geriatric units.

The Gap

Hospital MiC adoption is still below the level needed for a repeatable hospital development pipeline. DfMA can translate modular ambition into design rules, procurement logic, and evidence-based adoption pathways.

The Opportunity

Hospitals need speed, quality, infection-control reliability, adaptable layouts, and dense MEP coordination: conditions where manufacturable design and assembly planning matter most.
